Teaching

Artificial Neural Networks:

Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) have emerged as a cornerstone of modern machine learning and artificial intelligence. In this comprehensive course, we will delve into the core concepts, methodologies, and applications of ANNs, exploring their power in nonlinear modeling and their connection to kernel spaces. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), a specialized form of ANNs, will take center stage. We will explore their unique architecture, which has revolutionized image and visual data processing. Understanding CNNs is pivotal for tasks such as image recognition, object detection, and image generation. Moving forward, we will delve into Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), which excel at sequential data processing and have led to significant advancements in natural language understanding, machine translation, and speech recognition. We will explore the concept of memory in RNNs and its implications for processing time-series data.

Optimization Techniques in Artificial Intelligence

Optimization techniques have become a fundamental pillar of modern artificial intelligence and machine learning. In this comprehensive course, we will explore the core concepts, methodologies, and applications of optimization, examining their pivotal role in enhancing AI models and solving complex real-world problems. We begin with an introduction to optimization, covering its significance in AI and its applications across various domains. We will delve into the basic concepts, including objective functions, constraints, and feasible regions, laying a solid foundation for understanding more advanced topics. Next, we will explore convex sets and convex functions, which form the backbone of many optimization problems. Understanding the properties and visualizations of convex sets, as well as the characteristics of convex functions, is crucial for grasping the intricacies of optimization. Lagrange duality will be another focal point, offering insights into Lagrange multipliers, dual functions, and the weak and strong duality theorems. This understanding is essential for tackling constrained optimization problems effectively. The course will then dive into various convex optimization algorithms, including gradient descent, Newton's method, and quasi-Newton methods. We will discuss the practical considerations of these algorithms, such as convergence analysis and stopping criteria, supported by case studies and examples to illustrate their real-world applications. As we progress, we will explore advanced optimization techniques like second-order cone programming (SOCP), semidefinite programming, and semi-infinite programming. These methods are vital for solving complex optimization problems in areas such as finance, engineering, and artificial intelligence. Specialized optimization methods, including minimax optimization, sublinear algorithms, interior point methods, and active set methods, will be covered in detail. Additionally, we will examine the applications of stochastic gradient descent (SGD), coordinate descent, and the cutting planes method in various AI and machine learning tasks. The course will culminate in practical applications of optimization techniques to image, video, and multimedia processing. We will study case studies and practical implementations that showcase the power of optimization in enhancing AI models and solving real-world challenges. Throughout the course, you will engage in hands-on programming assignments and projects that align with the topics covered in the lectures, reinforcing your understanding and providing practical experience.

Research

Inverse and Ill-Posed Problems

Inverse problems constitute a fascinating realm where the objective is to discern the causal factors behind a given observation or effect. This field has been a subject of study for nearly a century, predominantly driven by practical applications. A pivotal characteristic, both from a theoretical and numerical standpoint, of inverse problems is their intrinsic ill-posed nature. Specifically, we term the problem :math:F(x) = f^{\delta} as ill-posed due to the fact that its solution lacks continuous dependence on the right-hand side. In other words, they deviate from Hadamard's classical prerequisites of solution existence, uniqueness, and stability in the face of perturbed data. The solutions to inverse problems may not universally exist for all conceivable data scenarios. For instance, a consistent temperature history is only viable for an exceedingly smooth final temperature in the classical heat equation model. Furthermore, uniqueness might not be guaranteed, thereby prompting the practical question of identifiability—whether the available data holds sufficient information to deduce the desired quantity. Additionally, instability in relation to data perturbations is a salient aspect. This becomes particularly crucial as real-world measurements invariably contain noise, with errors in numerical methods further amplifying the issue. As a remedy, regularization methods are devised to craft approximation techniques that are minimally influenced by noise. These techniques will be explored within the framework of "Emerging Applications of Inverse Problems Techniques to Signal Reconstruction and Imaging Science." The scope of these applications encompasses an array of challenges, including signal restoration or approximation from noisy data, tomography in medical imaging, microscopy, atmospheric imaging, biometrics, integrating optics and imaging, and computer vision methodologies. Moreover, applications extend to domains such as applied optics and remote sensing sciences. One pertinent example is atmospheric turbulence blur, which arises in applied optics and remote sensing due to prolonged exposure through the atmosphere. The turbulence within the atmosphere gives rise to random fluctuations in the refractive index, contributing to image degradation. In conclusion, the study of inverse and ill-posed problems not only delves into the intricate task of deciphering hidden causes but also navigates the complex interplay between data noise, instability, and the quest for accurate solutions. The diverse applications, ranging from medical imaging to atmospheric sciences, underscore the significance of addressing ill-posedness for the advancement of various fields.

Machine Learning

This pursuit, in statistical parlance, entails the art of learning from the everyday data tapestry that we weave. Machine Learning's significance transcends disciplinary boundaries, offering a transformative means of automating problem-solving processes on a grand scale. Positioned at the intersection of artificial intelligence and computer science, Machine Learning harnesses the power of data - a resource we generate in abundance. It finds kinship with the realms of statistics and mathematics, intertwining disparate disciplines into a harmonious tapestry. This harmonization is reflected in its ability to address diverse challenges spanning DNA analysis, medical diagnosis, product recommendations, stock trading, and credit card fraud detection. Machine Learning's roots trace back to the seeds of pattern recognition, but its evolution over the past decades has been nothing short of revolutionary. Its purview has expanded to enfold domains like computer vision, speech recognition, and natural language processing within the fold of artificial intelligence systems. Central to the learning process is the training of models to improve performance, a trio composed of the training data, learning algorithms, and test data. The pantheon of machine learning lies in its algorithms, a plethora of solutions crafted to tackle an array of challenges. It's pertinent to note that no single algorithm holds the key to all machine learning problems; rather, a mosaic of techniques is tailored to the task at hand. The paradigms of supervised learning, unsupervised learning, and reinforcement learning provide a framework for understanding machine learning's mechanisms. These categories, while serving as intellectual guideposts, often blend together in the real-world applications, reflecting the versatility of machine learning techniques. Today's computational milieu, epitomized by High-Performance Computing, transcends its initial role in computational modeling and simulation. It has seamlessly integrated the demands of big data processing, analysis, data mining, and machine learning. This transformation has not only led to computational prowess but also encompasses expertise in computational science, data science, and data management. The domain of machine learning is a dynamic frontier, where new formalizations of learning problems continually push its boundaries. A keen focus lies on refining algorithms, given the plethora of parameters they rely upon. Novel methods of parameter tuning promise significant efficiency gains. Additionally, distributed learning has emerged as a prominent trend, enabling machine learning algorithms to operate across distributed computing architectures. This evolution addresses resource constraints and facilitates the simultaneous processing of multiple hyperparameters, opening avenues for more intricate algorithms. Machine Learning's future holds promise as it inches towards emulating natural cognitive processes. It serves the national interest by bolstering fields like weather prediction, disaster response, and cyber and biosecurity. The tapestry of applications extends to agriculture, telecommunications, healthcare, and beyond. This synergy between human-like learning and technological innovation ensures Machine Learning's continued prominence as the modern-day solution to age-old challenges.
